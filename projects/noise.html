Electronics: Noise City

My final project for Physical Computing was a noise map of New York. The name is a play on John Zorn’s Naked City.

Brief: Noise is a part of city life. It is both a nuisance and a comfort. But, as major cities shift from slums to luxury playgrounds, a battle against noise is underway. In a recent lawsuit brought by a West Village resident against a bar/restaurant that is 80+ years old, the judge ruled in the bar’s favor. The judge’s argument stated, “In the modern world, some degree of noise, tension and discomfort is the inevitable concomitant of ­urban life.” I would like to highlight noise and let people experience the chaotic joy of many things producing sound by crashing into one another.

Audience: People who appreciate noise or who can at least tolerate it. Also people interested in an immersive exploration of New York City.

Testing: I was set on using strings because they would reference the streets of Manhattan as well as an instrument. The best looking solution, a stretch sensor, would not work because I wanted streets to be able to have different sounds at intersections with multiple avenues. Next, I tried breaking open the housing of a piezo sensor to make it more sensitive. The thinking was that vibration from a string would activate the sensor if the activation point was nearby. But, the piezo needed a mechanical vibration -- the string needed to touch the sensor to activate it.

My final solution was to use light sensors. A finger passing over the sensor while striking a string would alter the ambient light and activate the noise for that location.

Fabrication: I used Illustrator and a laser cutter to create the Manhattan grid. The horizontal streets are all in 10 block segments, except downtown where things get very diagonal. I decided to keep things either horizontal or vertical there so it could still be playable. But, because Broadway is such a major street, I allowed that to be diagonal and just located sounds at places where it intersected with an avenue.

The string is for a Flamenco guitar. The light sensors are glued into holes coming up from underneath the board.
